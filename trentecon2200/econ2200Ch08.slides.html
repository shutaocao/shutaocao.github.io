1. Point estimation¶

  • A point estimate is a single statistic computed from a random sample, used to estimate a population parameter.

  • Population parameter: quantities that characterize the population distribution.

  • Example. The expected value, $\mu$, of a population distribution is a population parameter, it can be estimated from a random sample $X_1, X_2,\cdots$. We can take the sample mean, $\overline{X}_n$, as an estimate of $\mu$. We write, $\widehat{\mu}=\overline{X}_n$.

  • Example: labor earnings again.
  • Any of the sample mean can be an estimate of $\mu$.
  • Point estimation of different population parameters when $N$ and $n$ are sufficiently large.

$p$ $\overline{p} = \frac{K}{n}$ $\sigma_{\overline{p}}^2 = \frac{p(1-p)}{n}$ $s_{\overline{p}}^2 =\frac{\overline{p}(1-\overline{p})}{n-1}$

Finite-population correction for point estimation¶

  • Estimates in the above slides are based on random sampling with replacement. Empirically it applies to when sample size $n$ is large, but still smaller than $N$, meaning $N$ should be sufficiently large too.

  • In many cases, random sampling is drawn without replacement, or population size $N$ is not large, it is finite.

  • In a finite population, the point estimation of population parameters is corrected by a factor $\frac{N-n}{N-1}$ or $\frac{N-n}{N}$, so called the finite-population correction.

  • Point estimation of different population parameters with finite-population correction.
Population Parameter Estimate of population parameter Variance of Estimate with correction Estimated variance with correction
$\mu$ $\overline{X}_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ $\sigma_{\overline{X}}^2 = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\left(\frac{N-n}{N-1}\right)$ $s_{\overline{X}}^2 =\frac{s^2}{n}\left(\frac{N-n}{N}\right)$
$\sigma^2$ $\frac{N-1}{N}s^2$

  • In the table, $s^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}[(X_1-\overline{X}_n)^2+(X_2-\overline{X}_n)^2+\cdots+(X_n-\overline{X}_n)^2]$.

  • NOTE: Formulas in the 4th column slightly differ from those on page 263 of textbook (formulas 8-6, 8-7, 8-8). Consider both are correct.

  • Why are these estimates? Can other estimates be used.

  • The estimates reported in above tables are not the only ones we can use. Importantly, the estimates reported in the tables are the "best" ones: they were proved to approach to true values of population parameters when sample size is sufficiently large.

  • Bias: the bias of the sample mean is defined as $\overline{X}_n-\mu$. Estimate is unbiased if $E[\overline{X}_n]-\mu=0$. This applies to any estimates of any population parameter.

  • Unbiasedness is one measure of the "best" estimates.

2. Confidence intervals¶

  • Point estimates are likely different from true values of parameters. We want to know how close they are to true values. We can use the confidence intervals.

  • A $100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence interval of the population mean $\mu$ is a random interval $[a,b]$ such that the value of $\mu$ lies in $[a,b]$ with probability $1- \alpha$. The random interval is computed from a random sample.

  • That is, random interval $[a,b]$ traps $\mu$ with probability $1-\alpha$.

  • E.g., a $95\%$ confidence interval for $\mu$ is a random interval $[a,b]$ that contains $\mu$ with probability 0.95. Here, $\alpha = 0.05$.

  • Interpretation: if we take many random samples, and form a confidence interval for each random sample, about $95\%$ of these intervals would contain $\mu$.

2-1. Confidence interval for $\mu$ when population standard deviation $\sigma$ is known¶

  • Recall, by the central limit theorem, $\frac{\overline{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma_{\overline{X}}}$ has approximately a standard normal distribution. $\overline{X}_n$ is the sample mean, and can be an estimate of $\mu$. $\sigma_{\overline{X}}$ is the standard deviation of the sampling mean, where $\sigma$ is the population standard deviation. Then $$P\left(-z_{\alpha}<\frac{\overline{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma_{\overline{X}}}<z_{\alpha}\right) = 1-\alpha.$$

  • Also recall, $1-\alpha$ is the area under the graph of normal probability density function between $-z_{\alpha}$ and $z_{\alpha}$.

  • For example, from the standard normal distribution table (Appendix B.1 of textbook), we know that $z_{\alpha}= 1.96$ if $\alpha=0.05$.

2-2. Confidence interval for $\mu$ when population standard deviation $\sigma$ is unknown¶

  • When the value of $\sigma^2$ is unknown, we can no longer use the confidence interval $\overline{X}_n \pm z_{\alpha} \sigma_{\overline{X}}$ for population mean $\mu$.

  • Fortunately, the quantity $\frac{\overline{X}_n - \mu}{s_{\overline{X}}}$ has the student's $t$ distribution or simply the $t$ distribution.

  • The $t$ distribution is symmetric about zero. It is similar to standard normal distribution.

  • The probability density function of a random variable following the $t$ distribution depends on the random sample size $n$. As $n$ goes to infinity, the $t$ distribution tends to the standard normal distribution.

  • Probability density function for standard normal random variables, for random variables with $t$ dsitribution
  • The $100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence interval for population mean $\mu$ is then $\overline{X}_n \pm t_{\alpha} \cdot s_{\overline{X}}$, where $t_{\alpha}$ satisfies $$P\left(-t_{\alpha}<\frac{\overline{X}_n - \mu}{s_{\overline{X}}}<t_{\alpha}\right) = 1-\alpha.$$

  • But this probability is the area under the graph of the probability density function for a random variable with the $t$ distribution.

  • Example. The $95\%$ confidence interval of population mean from a random sample of size $n=5$ is $\overline{X}_n \pm 2.776 \cdot s_{\overline{X}}$.

  • Refer to the tables in Section 1 of these slides for the expressions of $s_{\overline{X}}$. Again, Equation [8-7] on page 263 of textbook is also considered to be correct.

2-3. Confidence intervals for population fraction $p$¶

  • The $100(1-\alpha)\%$ confidence interval for population fraction is $\overline{p} \pm z_{\alpha} \cdot \sigma_{\overline{p}}$ when $p$ is known, and $\overline{p} \pm z_{\alpha} \cdot s_{\overline{p}}$ when $p$ is unknown.

  • The construction of these intervals are similar to those for the population mean, since $\overline{p}$ has approximately a normal distribution.

  • We follow the textbook, and use the standard normal distribution to construct the confidence intervals when $p$ is known and is unknown, unless explicitly asked.

2-4. Two examples of computing confidence intervals¶

  • Example 1. A survey company wants to determine the mean income of employees in the retail industry. The population is the income of all employees in that industry. A random sample of 361 employees reveals a sample mean of $\$54520$. The standard deviation of the income in the population is $\$3060$. Questions are:
    • What is the population mean? What is a reasonable value to use as an estimate of the population mean?
    • What is a reasonable range of values for the population mean?
    • What do these results mean?

  • From data, the population means is unknown, its estimate can be the sample mean $\overline{X}=\$54520$.

  • A "reasonable" range is subjective. Let us want to use the $95\%$ level of confidence, we can construct the $95\%$ confidence interval for population mean. Since the standard deviation of the income in population is known, the estimate of population mean has approximately a normal distribution. The $95\%$ confidence interval is then $54520 \pm 1.96 \cdot 3060/\sqrt{361}$ which is about $[54204, 54835]$.

  • The confidence interval implies that, if we select many random samples, each with a size of 361, from the population. For each random sample, we compute the sample mean as an estimate of the population mean, and we construct the $95\%$ confidence interval for each random sample. In $95\%$ of these random samples, the confidence intervals contain the population mean.

3. Appropriate sample size $n$¶

  • From the idea of the confidence interval, we can decide the sample size $n$.

  • We define the margin of error as $E_m = z_{\alpha} \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$.

  • To decide the sample size $n$, we first determine the size of $E_m$, then $$n = \left(\frac{z_{\alpha} \sigma}{E_m}\right)^2.$$

  • Here, $z_{\alpha}$ is again the standard normal value corresponding to the desired level of confidence $100(1-\alpha)\%$.

  • Example. Suppose one wants to survey a random sample of employees in the retail industry. The desired margin of error is determined to be at most $\$300$, further suppose that one knows the population standard deviation to be $\$3000$. What is the necessary size of the random sample?

  • This depends on the level of confidence, further again suppose that one wants the confidence level computed from the random sample to be $95\%$.

  • Then, $n = \left(\frac{1.96 \cdot 3000}{300}\right)^2 = 384$.
