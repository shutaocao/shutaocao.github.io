1. Measures of central tendency¶

  • Measures of central tendency, or central location, are often referred to as averages.

  • The purpose of a measure of location is to pinpoint the centre of a set of data.

  • An average is a measure of location that shows the central value of the data.

    • Example, the average number of hours high-school students spend playing video games each week.

  • Five measures of tendency

    • the arithmetic mean
    • the weighted mean
    • the median
    • the mode
    • the geometric mean

  • Each measure can be applied to both a population and a sample

1-1. Arithmetic mean¶

Population mean¶

  • Let $X$ be the variable of interest, e.g., the height of a high-school student in Canada in 2020.

  • Let $x_j$ be the value of $X$ for the $j$-th observation in population.

  • Let $N$ be the size of population, e.g., all high-school students in Canada in 2020.

  • Define $\mu$ the population arithmetic mean, e.g., the average height of high-school students in Canada in 2020.

  • Then $\mu = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^N x_j}{N} = \frac{1}{N}(x_1+x_2+x_3+...+x_{N-1}+x_N)$.

Example. NHL goals scored¶

  • Fourteen teams in the Eastern Conference of the NHL. Table shows the number of goals scored by each team in the 2013–2014 season.

  • Variable of interest, $X$, is the number of goals scored by a team in 2013/2014 season.

  • Observed values of $X$, $x_j$ is the number of goals scored by team $j$.

  • Population is all NHL teams in east conference, population size $N=14$.

  • The arithmetic mean is $\mu = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^{14} x_j}{14} = 215.71$.

Sample mean¶

  • Sample mean is defined similarly, $\overline{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i}{n}$, where $n$ is the sample size.

  • Example: Sample is 14 internet users and their reported numbers of minutes on social networks.

  • The arithmetic mean for this sample is $\overline{x} = \frac{1}{14}(85+93+105+87+120+97+120+79+114+107+115+88+109+113) = 102.3$.

1-2. Median¶

  • Median is the midpoint of the values in a set of numbers ordered from the minimum to the maximum.

  • If the data sample size is odd, median is the value separating the higher half and the lower half of the sample.

  • If the data sample size is even, median is the average of two values in the middle.

Example¶

  • Age of seven employees in a bank: 31, 35, 29, 30, 32, 33,34.

  • Order the values of the sample: 29, 30, 31, 32, 33, 34, 35.

  • Median age for the sample is 32.

  • Let's add another employee whose age is 35.

  • Median age of the sample of size 8 is (32+33)/2 = 32.5.

1-3. Mode¶

  • Mode is the value of the variable of interest that occurs with the most frequency.

  • In the above example, for the sample with size 8, the mode is 35.

1-4. Weighted mean in a sample¶

  • Weighted mean is the average of observed values in a sample where each observation has a weight, and the weight is applied to taking the average.

  • Let $X$ be the variable of interest, $x_i$ be the observed value of $X$ for the $i$-th observation, $w_i$ is the weight of the $i$-th observation, and $n$ be the sample size.

  • Weighted mean is $\overline{x}_w = \frac{w_1 x_1+ w_2 x_2 + ... + w_n x_n}{w_1+w_2+...+w_n}$, or equivalently $$\overline{x}_w = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n w_i x_i}{\sum_{i=1}^n w_i}.$$

Example¶

  • Grade of quiz in a class.
  • Sample size (number of observations) is $n=6$, population is $N = 48=(4+5+13+11+8+7)$.

  • Weighted mean is $\overline{x}_w = \frac{4\cdot 5 +5 \cdot 6 +13 \cdot 7 +11\cdot 8+8 \cdot 9 +7\cdot 10}{4+5+13+11+8+7} = 7.73$.

  • Arithmetic mean (for the sample of size 6) is $\overline{x} = \frac{5+6+7+8+9+10}{6} = 7.5$.

1-5. Geometric mean¶

  • Let $X$ be the variable of interest, $x_j$ be the value of $X$ for the observation $j$, in a population of size $N$.

  • The geometric mean of $X$ is defined as $$\mu_G = (x_1 \cdot x_2\cdot \dots \cdot x_N)^{\frac{1}{N}}.$$

  • We can also write it as $\mu_G = \left(\prod_{j=1}^N x_j\right)^{\frac{1}{N}}$.

  • Similarly, the geometric mean for a sample of size $n$ is $\overline{x}_G = \left(\prod_{i=1}^n x_i\right)^{\frac{1}{n}}$.

  • Geometric mean is more useful than arithmetic mean for measuring the average changes of percentages, ratios, indexes. It is also used for measuring average growth rates over time.

  • (Optional) For your information. If $x_i>0$ for all $i$, then $\overline{x}_G \le \overline{x}$, geometric mean is smaller than arithmetic mean; They equal if $x_i=x>0$ for all $i$.

  • Example. Rate of return on investment in firm ABC for 5 successive years was: 20%, 30%, -30%, 50%, 300%.

  • The geometric mean rate of gross return is $\overline{x}_G = (1.2\cdot 1.3 \cdot 0.7 \cdot 1.5 \cdot 4.0)^{1/5}=1.4564$, the rate of net return is 45.64% per year.

  • Arithmetic mean rate of gross return is $\overline{x} =\frac{1}{5}(1.2+ 1.3 + 0.7 + 1.5 + 4.0) = 1.74$.

  • Another use of geometric mean is to measure average growth rates. Let $x_1$ be the value of $X$ in year 1, $x_T$ be the value of $X$ in year $T$.

  • The average yearly growth rate over the $T$ years is $\left(\frac{x_T}{x_1} \right)^{\frac{1}{T-1}} -1$.

  • To see why, write $\mu_G = \left(\frac{x_2}{x_1}\cdot \frac{x_3}{x_2}\cdot \frac{x_4}{x_3}\cdot \cdots\cdot \frac{x_T}{x_{T-1}}\right)^{\frac{1}{T-1}} = \left(\frac{x_T}{x_{1}}\right)^{\frac{1}{T-1}}$.

  • $\frac{x_t}{x_{t-1}}$ is the gross growth rate from year $t-1$ to year $t$.

2. Measures of Dispersion¶

  • Dispersion of a variable of interest tells how far it is between the low and high values.

  • It is of significant interest and importance, for example, dispersion in labor income, gender gaps in education and income, etc.

  • Many measures, we study

    • Range
    • Mean deviation
    • Variance; standard deviation
    • Coefficient of variation

  • Notation: $X$ is the variable of interest, $x_i$ or $x_j$ is the value of $X$ for observation $i$ or $j$, $n$ is the sample size, $N$ is the population size.

2-1. Range and Mean deviation¶

  • Range is the maximum value minus the minimum value in a sample or population

  • Mean deviation (MD) of $X$ in a sample is defined as $\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n | x_i- \overline{x}|} {n}$.

  • Example: MD = 110/6 = 18.33

2-2. Variance and standard deviation¶

  • Variance and standard deviation are both slightly different between population and sample.

  • Population variance is $\sigma^2 = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^N (x_j - \mu)^2}{N}$, where $\mu$ is the population arithmetic mean.

  • Population standard deviation is the square root of population variance, $\sigma = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{j=1}^N (x_j - \mu)^2}{N}}$.

  • Terms $x_j - \mu$ are called residuals, deviation from the mean.

  • Sample variance is $s^2 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \overline{x})^2}{n-1}$, where $\overline{x}$ is the sample arithmetic mean.

  • Sample standard deviation is the square root of population variance, $s = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \overline{x})^2}{n-1}}$.

  • Different from $\sigma^2$ and $\sigma$, here we use $n-1$ instead of $n$ in the denominator.

  • FYI: Using $n-1$ instead of $n$ is called the Bessel's correction, it corrects the bias of estimating population variance $\sigma^2$ by using $s^2 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \overline{x})^2}{n}$. Why the bias? There are $n$ independent observations in the sample, but there are only $n - 1$ independent residuals, because $\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i -\overline{x}) = 0$.

  • Sample variance can also be written as $s^2 = \frac{(\sum_{i=1}^n x_i^2) - \frac{1}{n}(\sum_{i=1}^n x)^2}{n-1}$.

Example: prices of Jelly

  • Sample variance is $s^2 = \frac{1680 - 90 \cdot 90 / 5}{5-1}=15\$^2$.

Chebyshev's inequality¶

  • In math, for a random variable $X$ with mean value $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, for any number $k>0$, $$\text{Prob}(|X -\mu|\ge k \sigma) \le \frac{1}{k^2}.$$

  • Statistically and roughly, in words, the proportion of the values that lie within $k$ standard deviations of the mean is at least $1-1/k^2$, where $k>1$.

  • For a symmetrical bell-shaped frequency distribution:

    • Approximately 68% of observations lie within $\pm 1$ standard deviation of the mean.
    • About 95% of observations lie within $\pm 2$ standard deviations of the mean.
    • About 99.7% lie within $\pm 3$ standard deviations of the mean.
  • A symmetrical bell-shaped curve showing the relationship between the standard deviation and the observations

2-3. Coefficient of variation¶

  • Variances and standard deviations have units (e.g., dollars, meters), making them difficult to compare among different variables.

  • We want to normalize the dispersion measure, by dividing them by the mean.

  • The coefficient of variation is the ratio of the standard deviation over the arithmetic mean, expressed as a percentage, $CV = \frac{s}{\overline{x}}$.

  • Coefficient of variation allows us to make a meaningful comparison of different standard deviations.

3. Skewness¶

  • Skewness measures the degree of asymmetry of the distribution of observed values.

  • A set of values is skewed to the right or positively skewed if there is a single peak and the values extend much further to the right of the peak than to the left of the peak.

    • In this case the mean is larger than the median.

  • In a negatively skewed distribution there is a single peak but the observations extend further to the left, in the negative direction, than to the right.

    • The mean is smaller than the median.
  • Shapes of Frequency Polygons

Pearson's Coefficient of Skewness¶

  • Pearson's coefficient of skewness is $sk = \frac{3(\text{mean}-\text{median})}{\text{standard deviation}}$.

  • Another measure of skewness is $sk = \frac{n}{(n-1)(n-2)}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n \left(\frac{x_i - \overline{x}}{s}\right)^3\right]$.

Example. Earnings per share, in dollars, for a sample of 16 stocks in some year.

  • Mean is 5.76, standard deviation is 5.85. Median is 3.52.

  • Pearson's coefficient of skewness is $\frac{3(5.76 - 3.52)}{5.85}=1.15$. Distribution displays a positive skewness.

4. Relative positions¶

  • The standard deviation is the most widely used measure of dispersion.

  • There are other ways of describing the variation or spread in a set of data.

  • One method is to determine the location of values that divide a set of observations into equal parts,

    • they include quartiles, deciles, and percentiles.

  • Quartiles divide a set of observations into four equal parts.

  • Deciles divide a set of observations into ten equal parts.

  • Percentiles divide a set of observations into 100 equal parts.

  • For a number of observations, $n$, the location of the $P$th percentile is $$L_p = (n+1)\frac{P}{100}.$$

Example

  • Commissions earned by a sample of 17 real estate agents in a particular month.

  • Locations of $P$th percentile for different values of $P$ are

    • $L_{25} = (17+1) \frac{25}{100} = 4.5$, whose commission is $\$1967$.

    • $L_{50} = (17+1) \frac{50}{100} = 9.0$, whose commission is $\$2001$.

    • $L_{75} = (17+1) \frac{75}{100} = 13.5$, whose commission is $\$2345$.

6. Descriptive statistics for grouped data¶

  • Recall, some data are grouped, with frequency for each group. For example

  • For example, list prices of real estate in Edmonton

  • In the example, each class is an interval of prices. We need to use the midpoints.

  • The arithmetic mean of grouped data is defined as $\overline{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n f_i x_i}{n}$,

    • where $f_i$ is the frequency falling into class $i$, $x_i$ is the midpoint value of class $i$, $n$ is sample size.

  • In the above example, $\overline{x} = \frac{43125}{96} = \$449.2 \ \text{thousand}$.

  • This resembles but differs from the weighted arithmetic mean for continuous data.

  • Median of grouped data for population data is based on the median class, i.e., the class that the middle observation falls into.

  • It is defined as $\text{Median} = L + \frac{N/2 - f^c}{f} \cdot i$. Strange notation.

  • Here, $L$ is the lower limit (boundary) of the median class

  • $N$ is the population size (number of observations)
  • $f$ is the frequency of the median class
  • $f^c$ is the cumulative frequency up to but excluding the median class

  • $i$ is the class width of the median class

  • For sample data, replace $N$ with sample size $n$.

Example 1. Marks of a quiz in a course with 30 students.

  • Order the data.

  • The position of the median mark $L_{50} = (30+1)\frac{50}{100} = 15.5$, so the median class is 7.

  • In the median class, the mark is 7, so the median mark is 7.

  • No need to apply to the above formula, since it does not have an interval.

Example 2. List prices on an area of a city.

  • Order observations.

  • Find the position of the median price, $L_p = (n+1)\frac{P}{100} = (98+1)\frac{50}{100} = 49.5$.

  • Median class is "400 to under 800".

  • Median price is in the class where the 49.5th observation falls into.

  • Median price then equal $L + \frac{n/2 - f^c}{f} \cdot i = 400 + \frac{98/2-40}{42} \cdot 400 = 485.7$.

  • Standard deviation of grouped data is defined as $$s = \sqrt{\frac{(\sum_{i=1}^n f_i x_i^2) - \frac{(\sum_{i=1}^n f_ix_i)^2}{n}}{n-1}}.$$

  • Here, $f_i$ is the frequency of class $i$, $x_i$ is the midpoint value of class $i$, $n$ is the sample size (total number of frequencies).

Example. List price of real estates in an area of city ABC.

  • Apply the formula, we have $s = \sqrt{\frac{21562500 - \frac{43125^2}{96}}{96-1}} = 151.83$.
