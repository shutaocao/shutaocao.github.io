1. Some Facts of Trade Patterns¶

  • Remarkable growth of world trade since WW II. The ratio of trade (exports+imports) to GDP has increased.
    • World trade as a share of world GDP.

  • Trade in manufactured goods and services has been growing at a particularly fast pace.

    • Merchandise exports were 16.0 trillion USD in 2016.
    • Exports of services amounted to 4.0 trillion USD in 2016.

  • Leading traded products are computers, motor cars, petroleum, clothing, ...

  • Large exporters/importers are China, US, Germany, Japan, and UK.

    • They accounted for more than 1/3 of world trade in 2015.

  • Top ten merchandise trading countries accounted for 52% of world total trade in 2015.

    • This share for service trade is 53% in 2015.

  • Exports to GDP ratios differ across countries:

    • 24% for low-income countries, 37% for middle-income countries, and 42% for high-income countries.

  • About half of the world trade is among the advanced economies.

  • The share of intra-inudstry trade is large (over 60% of total trade).

  • Only a small fraction of firms export.

  • Fragmentation of production has been a major factor in the growth of trade and FDI.
    • About 1/3 of world trade is intra-firm (Helpman 2016).
    • Global production: Boeing 787 Dreamliner (Antras 2015)

2. Why do we study international trade¶

  • Why do countries trade with each other?
  • How do exports and imports trade affect national income?
  • Which trade policies are good, for whom?
  • How are trade policies determined?
  • what explain that some firms participate in international trade but others do not?
  • Why and how are production processes fragmented across countries?
  • How does foregin trade affect income of various groups (inequality)?

4. What do we study?¶

  • Theories explaining the patterns of international trade.

  • Quantification of gains from international trade.

  • Optimal policy on international trade.

  • Firm heterogeneity in international trade, (if time permits).

Course overview¶

  • Advanced International Trade: Theory and Evidence

    • Neoclassical trade theory: Ricardian model; Heckscher-Onlin model.

    • New trade theory: Increasing returns to scale, monopolistic competition.

    • Gravity equation, explaining trade flows.

    • Gains from trade.

    • Trade policy and the political economy of trade policy.

    • Trade and Firm heterogeneity.

      • Global value chains: offshoring (other names: outsourcing, fragmentation).

Prerequisites?
¶

  • Microeconomic theory: producer theory; consumer theory; general equilibrium.

  • Econometric methods: linear models; discrete choice; MLE; GMM; sample selection; endogeneity.

  • (Undergraduate trade theory).
